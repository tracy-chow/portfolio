Dip Dip Sushi Ordering App

When you simply can't stomach another night of mac n' cheese to avoid fighting with the kids. Reframing the way families have meals together. Assignment for Google UX Design Professional Certificate.

Overview

The Product

A menu and ordering app for a sushi restaurant was created as part of my capstone project for my Google UX Design Professional Certificate. The interface should be easy to use, appeal to a broad audience (especially children), and encourage people to try sushi by allowing them to customize their orders, filter out allergens, and learn about what they are eating.

Project Duration

April 2022 - September 2022

‍

Problem

Sushi is not mainstream and this cuts into the restaurant’s revenues. Customers are hesitant to try something that’s unfamiliar and have difficulty customizing to their preferences or dietary restrictions.

‍

Goal

Opening up a new segment of the market for sushi restaurants by making it easy to order, customize, and educate customers about their sushi orders.

‍

My Role

UX designer & UX researcher for a Dip Dip Sushi app from concept to delivery. This project was also (unconsciously) inspired by a research study I conducted many years ago on children's thinking on nutrition. With the level of obesity rising in children, it is important that we create ways for children to eat more healthfully. Understanding the Users

‍

User Research: Summary

Four people who order meals using a web-based and/or mobile app at least once a week for themselves and at least one other person were interviewed to determine the pain points they experience when deciding to order sushi. Based on Google Trends data from 2004 - present, “sushi” ranks significantly lower (almost 400 %) as a search term than “pizza” in the United States.

‍

‍

‍

User Research: Pain Points

‍This user group confirmed the initial assumptions about pizza being more popular as a takeout/delivery than sushi. Research also revealed that many are unfamiliar with sushi and thei ingredients; this made it difficult for customers who have specific food preferences or dietary needs. It also seems to be one of the major factors for families with children who would rather order pizza because they don’t have time to decipher or research the particular sushi before ordering with cost being one of the factors in ordering something their children may not want to eat.

• PICKY EATERS: There aren’t many options for picky eaters: Most children are not adventurous eaters and are often difficult when asked to try new foods they’ve never seen before. 

• UNFAMILIARITY: People may not know what to try or order because they don’t know the Japanese name listed or its ingredients.

• COST: The cost of sushi compared to pizza can be significant for a family. Leftovers cannot be saved like you can with pizza.

• ACCESSIBILITY: It’s difficult to order and/or customize sushi for food preferences, dietary needs, or age groups.

‍

User Personas:

‍

User Journey Maps

As many people with children can relate, Lisa wants something quick, easy, and affordable. She wants something she can enjoy as well but not have to have a battle with her children about. Busy people want something quick but special that can be enjoyed as an affordable way to spend time with loved ones. When you have food allergies, you want to know the place you go to is reliable about taking your allergies seriously. Once you have that trust, you will be a repeat customer since it requires less mental effort than to eat elsewhere.

‍

‍Hypothesis Statements

• If Lisa uses the sushi app to order dinner using the children’s mode, she can get her children to learn more about what they are eating to encourage them try a variety of food.

‍

Value Propositions

• Encourage people to try sushi and broaden their palettes, especially kids

• Ability to view ingredients of each type of sushi to make the anxiety of trying something new more manageable.

• Ability to filter sushi against a list of known allergens or allergens provided by the user to ensure food safety.

• Ability to customize order as needed to enable happy meal times.

Themes

Both adult and child users were recruited to guide the designs from wireframes to the first mockups. Children were observed while ordering & rating sushi from the the kid-friendly UI. Adults were observed as they created accounts, set up allergens, and ordered items from the app. The second study used a high-fidelity prototype to determine what needed refining.

‍

Round 1 Findings:

• Users want a simpler interface (less icons, visuals, etc).

• Users want to be able to make a  one-click order.

• Users want to be able to set up “order profiles” to reduce # of steps.

‍

Round 2 Findings:

• Colour palette was difficult for some users.

• The drawer menu wasn’t necessary for the kids UI.

• Movement to indicate items being added to the cart needed.

Takeaways & Next Steps

‍

‍

Impact & Outcomes

The app encourages users to try new foods by introducing the food through education. Children, especially, are encouraged to try new foods by being able to choose their own items that are easily curated by their grown-ups.

‍

One quote from a child user:

"So do I get more stars and more pets when I try another sushi? That's so cool! Do you get pets for the other stuff too? Or is it just for sushi?"

‍

What I Learned

Over several iterations of the app, from initial wireframes to high-fidelity prototype, I learned the importance of simplicity in technology. Additionally, it is important to get a diverse set of users for usability testing to help developers and designers address any usability blindspots.

‍

Next Steps

• Another round of usability studies with a more diverse set of children and families (and a larger sample size) would enable a better designed app.

• Factors that predict KPIs (e.g., how often a child tries a new sushi), can also be examined via regression or factor analysis.

• Enabling children to have a say in what they eat does not only affect mealtimes, it also empowers them to try new foods by learning more about them. Once the predictors can be found and used to motivate them to try new foods, the app can be expanded to other cuisines.

• Food is a way for people to share their cultures and traditions. It is also something that everyone has experienced. By encouraging and scaffolding children (and some adults) to try out new foods can help prevent health issues such as obesity and diabetes.

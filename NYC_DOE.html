NYC DOE in 1-2-3

Helping New Yorkers find the right public school in a New York minute... because public school education should be education for all. Assignment for Google UX Design Professional Certificate: UX Design for Social Impact.

Product Overview

1. Problem

Public school admissions in NYC is a complicated process; the school your child ends up being admitted to is affected not only by school choice but also by the priorities each student has based on location, siblings, test-scores, lottery, and others. In fact, the DOE has a 64-page PDF for families seeking admissions into the DOE. And though they are offered in a number of languages, families with the means end up hiring $250/hour consultants to help them focus on the schools they should apply to.

To ensure the public school system’s mission to provide quality education for all, students and their families should have access to what is available regardless of their socio-economic or education levels.

‍

2. Goal

In keeping with the spirit of providing equitable education for all NYC students, this app aims to demystify the admissions process with 5 simple questions to help families focus their time and energy in applying to schools to match their child’s needs.

‍

3. The Product 

DOE in 1-2-3 is an app to complement the New York City Department of Education’s MySchools dashboard for applying to NYC public schools. The app aims to demystify & simplify the complicated admissions system based on various levels of priorities to determine a student’s admission to the school of their choice.

Understanding Users

‍

1. User Research: Summary

New York City’s Department of Education is the largest school district in the United States with 1,058,888 student enrolled in 1,859 schools for the 2021-2022 school year. The Economic Need Index (ENI) estimates the percentage of students facing economic hardship, with a maximum value of 1.0 if the student has all of the following: eligible for public assistance, lived in temporary housing for the past 4 years, and has a home language other than English; the average ENI in 2021 is 0.71, meaning socio-economic needs are high in NYC. Since education is an extremely effective way to escape the cycles of poverty, access to quality education is crucial but the complicated process perpetuates this inequality if students cannot attend a school to meet their needs.

Ideation

‍

To address the complexity and the amount of information presented to families in all three apps, I focused on modeling my app around an app called “Turbo Tax”. Through a series of questions to determine the applicant’s priority in the admissions queue, the app aims to narrow the list with info only for schools they qualify for.

5. Accessibility Considerations & High Fidelity Prototype

Because NYC has the biggest school board in the USA, the number of users for this app is quite diverse and many do not speak the language. As such, the Google Translate API was embedded to enable real-time translations as well as to minimize the maintenance needed.

Symbols & icons were used alongside captions to allow for screen readers to read the text.

Colours were added in consultation with WAG standards to enable those with colour blindness to view the info. Shapes, in addition to colours, were also used to denote various categories.

These elements were incorporated into the high-fidelity prototype.

Outcomes & Takeaways

‍

1. Impact

A user likened the app and website to a Swiss army knife; it allows you to do what you need to do without the extra frills overwhelming the user. Users found the info more manageable when presented in chunks that aligned with the priority system of admissions used by the NYC Department of Education.

‍

2. What I Learned

Because the human mind can only process a small amount of information at a time, UIs need to be designed with that in mind to reduce cognitive overload. For most, if not all, users, an exhaustive search of all the NYC schools is completely unnecessary. Most only need the schools they qualify for and we aimed to do that with this app to make the information accessible by all.

‍

‍

Next Steps

Because the app has been designed using the branding guidelines from the NYC DOE MySchools dashboard, I would attempt to use the API for NYC Open Data to populate actual school information to run usability testing on a larger scale.

The app can easily be scale up to other divisions and age group. Though I focused on K-5 admissions as my division of choice, the app could make it possible for a family with students at different grade levels to use one single app for their research roadmap.

If possible, I would contact the mayor’s office to see if I could work with the DOE’s IT team to see if this is something that we can pilot. This would ensure all families have free & equal access to the information since consultants charge $250/hour to families who can afford to have someone synthesize the information for them.

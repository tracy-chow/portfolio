The Teacher's Toolbox

An analysis and reimagining of Scholastic's multi-platform interoperability to leverage the power of platform interoperability with usable information to elevate the superpowers of educators

Product Overview

1. Project Duration

September 2022 - October 2022

Further improvements were made based on my discussions with the directors of design and learning at Scholastic in January 2023

‍

2. Credits & Background Note

This UX project was inspired by my preparation for a possible position at Scholastic that eventually became the Teacher's Toolbox app for one of my assignments in a UX course. Screenshots of the individual activities from Scholastic's literacy programs were used to demonstrate the interoperability features of the platform.

“Scholastic publishes and distributes children's books, connecting educators and families through accessibility, engagement, and expertise.” (Crunchbase). With a number of acquisitions of digital platforms, Scholastic is looking to build a single platform that would enable its users (K-5 teachers) to access information, assessment, and data across all of their various apps.

‍

3. Problem

Platform interoperability is difficult for a variety of reasons. One of them involves the sharing of data between the platforms to ensure consistent protocols are used for data sharing. In addition, creating an all-in-one suite of products lowers the learning curve - enabling teachers to focus on their students. That's what this product sets out to do - making information accessible to teachers.

‍

4. Goal

A system and UI will need to be developed to enable meaningful and seamless integration between platforms.

Outcomes & Takeaways

1. Impact

The app enables teachers to have all the tools and info they need at their fingertips. It also allows the users to use a combination of tools to help students develop their literacy skills without needing to use several apps and then gather and synthesize the data for next steps.

‍

One quote from an educator:

"I often wonder why tech companies create tools that are so difficult to use. Have they tried being in a classroom full of kindergarteners all day long? A tool made in close consultation with an educator is sorely needed. You really can't understand the day-to-day reality from a one-off study."

‍

2. What I Learned

Over several iterations of the app, from initial wireframes to high-fidelity prototype, I learned the importance of simplicity in technology. Screen real-estate is valuable. Teachers don't have time to scroll when they are with their students. As such, responsive design and the use of drop down or hidden menus can help teachers prioritize the information they need but have access to the ones they don't need right away. Additionally, it is important to get a diverse set of users for usability testing to help developers and designers address any usability blindspots.

‍

3. Next Steps

• Teachers who teach languages that are offered on these platforms can increase subscription numbers for the company. Using Google Translate's API can ensure quicker testing and deployment.

• A student app could be useful for teachers to push out activities instantly.

• Though a dark mode is currently available, a light mode could be useful for various classroom environments.

• An accessibility menu can be added to increase font size, contrast, etc.
